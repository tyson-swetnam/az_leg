# Arizona State Government: Complete 2025 Membership Guide (with Ages)

Arizona operates under divided government in 2025, with Democratic Governor Katie Hobbs facing Republican majorities in both legislative chambers. The **57th Arizona Legislature**, which convened January 13, 2025, comprises **17-13 Republican control** in the Senate and **33-27** in the House. This guide provides the complete roster of all 90 legislators, 35 appellate judges, and key executive officials shaping Arizona policy, **updated with ages where available**.

---

## Executive Summary of Arizona Government Structure

Arizona's government follows the traditional three-branch model established by its 1912 constitution. The **legislative branch** is bicameral, with 30 senators and 60 representatives serving two-year terms from 30 districts (one senator and two representatives per district). The **executive branch** lacks a lieutenant governor—Arizona is one of only four states without one—placing the Secretary of State first in succession. The **judicial branch** uses merit selection for appellate courts, with judges appointed by the governor from commission-nominated lists and retained through periodic elections.

**Term limits** restrict legislators to four consecutive two-year terms (eight years) per chamber, after which they must wait one full term before returning. Governors are limited to two consecutive four-year terms. The **Arizona Independent Redistricting Commission**, a five-member citizen panel created by 2000's Proposition 106, draws legislative boundaries—current maps adopted January 2022 are based on 2020 Census data.

## Arizona Judicial Branch: Complete 2025 Roster

Arizona's judiciary uses **merit selection** for appellate courts—the Commission on Appellate Court Appointments (16 bipartisan members) nominates candidates, and the governor appoints from that list. Judges then face retention elections: an initial two-year term, followed by six-year terms if retained. **Mandatory retirement** is age 70.

## Term Limits and Electoral Mechanics

| Position | Term Length | Term Limit |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| State legislators | 2 years | 4 consecutive terms per chamber (8 years) |
| Governor | 4 years | 2 consecutive terms |
| Supreme Court justices | 6 years (after initial 2-year term) | Retention elections; mandatory retirement at 70 |
| Court of Appeals judges | 6 years (after initial 2-year term) | Retention elections; mandatory retirement at 70 |

Legislative term limits, enacted by voters through **Proposition 107 in 1992**, first took effect in 2000. After reaching limits in one chamber, legislators commonly run for the other chamber immediately. Annual legislative salary remains **$24,000** plus per diem—unchanged since 1998.

## Conclusion

Arizona's 2025 government reflects the state's competitive political landscape—a Democratic governor navigating Republican legislative majorities, with the judiciary shaped predominantly by Republican appointments from the Ducey era. The **17-13 Senate** and **33-27 House** margins give Republicans control but fall short of veto-proof supermajorities, ensuring continued negotiation between branches. 

Notable developments include the historic appointment of Justice Maria Elena Cruz (age ~53), the elevation of Steve Montenegro (age 43–44) as the first Hispanic House Speaker, and the upcoming creation of a lieutenant governor position in 2026. The independent redistricting commission's 2022 maps will govern elections through 2030, while the state's term limits continue to drive regular turnover and cross-chamber movement among experienced legislators.

The age range of Arizona's leadership spans from 39-year-old Senate President Pro Tem T.J. Shope to 79-year-old Superintendent Tom Horne, reflecting both generational turnover and the state's term limits forcing experienced legislators to seek different offices.
